# Ch 16 · Long-term therapy: bone, kidney, and metabolic management

> Medication for life isn't "swallow and forget."

---

## A premise

Before NUCs existed, the 10-year survival rate for chronic HBV was **catastrophic**. Today, a first-line patient on standard therapy has a life expectancy close to normal.

That in itself is a miracle.

But "close to normal" isn't "completely normal." **Long-term therapy has a cost. You need to see it and manage it.**

---

## Three focus areas

- **Kidney** — especially TDF users
- **Bone** — especially TDF users and older adults
- **Metabolism** — especially TAF users

---

## Kidney function: TDF's "old headache"

TDF is cleared by the kidneys, and long-term use can damage the **renal tubules**.

**Signs:**

- Slow decline in eGFR
- Elevated urinary microalbumin
- **Increased phosphate excretion** (important, directly linked to bone density)
- Rare severe case: Fanconi syndrome (widespread proximal tubular dysfunction)

**Monitoring:**

| Test | Frequency |
|------|-----------|
| Serum creatinine + eGFR | Every 6–12 months |
| Urinalysis (urine protein) | Yearly |
| Serum phosphate (high-risk) | Yearly |

**Warning thresholds:**

- eGFR < 60 mL/min/1.73m² — consider switching to TAF
- Persistent urine protein (+) or higher — needs evaluation
- **eGFR < 50 on two consecutive checks** — strong recommendation to switch

---

## Bone density: another TDF concern

TDF causes renal phosphate wasting, which indirectly affects bone:

- Bone density decreases
- Slight increase in hip and vertebral fracture risk
- But **clinical significance is limited in most cases**

**High-risk groups:**

- Postmenopausal women
- Men > 60
- Existing osteoporosis diagnosis
- Long-term glucocorticoid users
- Low body weight

**Monitoring:**

- High-risk patients get a baseline **DEXA scan**
- Recheck every 2–3 years
- T-score < -2.5 → osteoporosis, consider switching to TAF + calcium/VD supplementation

**Daily protection:**

- Calcium + VD supplementation (especially in older adults)
- Weight-bearing exercise
- Quit smoking, limit alcohol

---

## TAF's metabolic cost

TAF is kidney- and bone-friendly, but it has another issue — **blood lipids**.

Clinical trials found:

- After switching to TAF, **LDL and total cholesterol go up by 20–30 mg/dL on average**
- Some patients need lifestyle or drug intervention
- Whether long-term cardiovascular risk goes up — **data is still being collected**

**Monitoring:**

- Yearly lipid panel (TC, LDL, HDL, TG)
- Fasting glucose + HbA1c
- Blood pressure

**If LDL rises significantly:**

- Adjust diet and exercise first
- Start a statin if needed
- Rarely, switch back to TDF (weigh the trade-offs)

---

## Side effect comparison of the three drugs

| Side effect | ETV | TDF | TAF |
|-------------|-----|-----|-----|
| Kidney toxicity | Very low | Moderate | Very low |
| Bone density loss | Very low | Moderate | Very low |
| Elevated lipids | Very low | Very low | **Moderate** |
| GI discomfort | Rare | Occasional | Rare |
| Rare severe | Lactic acidosis | Fanconi syndrome | Not yet common |

**Idealized choice: young, no kidney/bone issues, can afford it → TAF; older with kidney/bone risk → also TAF; genuinely tight budget → generic TDF or ETV**

---

## What else to keep an eye on long-term

Beyond kidney, bone, and metabolism, long-term patients should regularly assess:

**① Antiviral response**
- Is DNA still undetectable
- Is quantitative HBsAg dropping
- HBeAg seroconversion status

**② Liver status**
- Long-term ALT/AST trend
- FibroScan every 1–2 years (has fibrosis reversed)
- Ultrasound + AFP every 6 months

**③ Whole-body health**
- Blood pressure, blood glucose, weight
- Blood lipids
- Bone density (high-risk)
- Cardiovascular risk assessment (especially TAF users)

---

## Compliance: the most overlooked issue

Surveys show that among people on chronic medication, **compliance (percentage of doses taken on time) is only 60–80%**. That means a fair share of patients **aren't actually getting the full drug effect**.

**Tips:**

- Phone alarm + pillbox **somewhere you see every day**
- Get 3–6 months of medication at once, avoid frequent pharmacy trips
- Carry a month's spare on business trips
- Family reminders help
- Apps can track, but they may be more trouble than they're worth for older users

**A single missed dose isn't a big deal, but repeated misses are a breeding ground for resistance and rebound.**

---

## A real-life lesson

A 55-year-old male patient took TDF for 8 years. The first 5 years were fine, then a checkup found:

- eGFR dropped from 90 to 62
- Bone density T-score = -2.1 (osteopenia)
- Urine protein (+)

The doctor switched him to TAF. One year later:

- eGFR stable at 70
- Bone density slightly better
- **But LDL rose from 2.8 to 3.8 mmol/L**

He's now on a statin, and LDL is back to 2.5.

**Lesson: there's no perfect drug, only a matched plan. Monitor regularly, adjust promptly.**

## Before stopping, talk

At some point some patients ask: "Can I stop the medication?"

**This is a complex question**, and the next chapter is dedicated to it. Here's the principle up front:

> **Stopping = a completely separate decision. It's not as simple as "my liver's fine, I'll quit."**

Stopping can bring:
- Virological rebound (nearly inevitable, unless functional cure is reached)
- Immune flare (sometimes severe)
- Liver failure risk (small but real)

**Any stopping must be doctor-guided + closely monitored.**

---

## 📍 Key Points

- Three focus areas of long-term therapy: **kidney, bone, metabolism**
- **TDF users**: yearly kidney function + urine protein; bone density in high-risk
- **TAF users**: yearly lipids + metabolic markers
- Three-drug vs side effect quick table (see above)
- **Compliance is the most overlooked issue** — one miss doesn't matter, repeated misses are dangerous
- Stopping is a completely separate decision, don't do it on your own
