# Ch 13 · Imaging

> What blood tests can't see, imaging can.

---

## Why imaging matters

Blood tests tell you **the state of the virus and liver function**.
Imaging tells you **what the liver looks like and whether there are abnormal nodules**.

For hepatitis B patients, the core job of imaging is — **early detection of hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)**.

Because HBV-related HCC has a signature trait: **it can appear suddenly even when liver function is normal and fibrosis isn't advanced**. **Many patients live with it hanging over their heads.**

Early-detected HCC has a 5-year survival rate above 70%; late-detected, below 20%. **That gap is the value of screening.**

---

## The four main tools

| Tool | Pros | Cons | Use |
|------|------|------|------|
| **Abdominal ultrasound** | Cheap, no radiation, convenient | Operator-dependent, poor images for obese patients | **First-line screening** |
| **Contrast CT** | High spatial resolution, fast | Radiation, contrast agent | Nodule characterization |
| **Contrast MRI** | Most sensitive, no radiation | Expensive, slow | Small nodule characterization, complex cases |
| **AFP + imaging** | Double safety net | Negative AFP doesn't rule out cancer | Standard combo |

---

## Ultrasound: entry-level screening

At each follow-up, get an abdominal ultrasound.

**What it looks at:**

- Liver size and shape
- Whether the surface is smooth (rough = cirrhosis sign)
- Parenchymal echogenicity (coarse, uneven = fibrosis)
- **Nodules** — presence, size, number, location
- Portal vein width (> 12 mm suggests portal hypertension)
- Spleen size (splenomegaly suggests portal hypertension)
- Gallbladder, ascites

**Suggested frequency for HBV patients:**

- Chronic HBV without cirrhosis: **every 6 months**
- Cirrhosis: **every 3-4 months**
- Stable on nucleoside therapy: **every 6 months**

---

## What to do if ultrasound finds a nodule

There's an international workflow for nodules:

**< 1 cm** — repeat ultrasound every 3 months to watch for growth

**1-2 cm** — do **contrast CT or contrast MRI** to check enhancement pattern

**> 2 cm or highly suspicious** — contrast CT/MRI immediately

---

## Contrast CT: the standard for nodule characterization

**"Contrast" means injecting contrast agent and imaging in several phases.**

The **typical enhancement pattern** for HCC:

- **Arterial phase (20-40 seconds after injection)** — the nodule enhances markedly (brighter than surrounding liver)
- **Portal venous phase (60-90 seconds)** — the nodule "washes out," becoming darker than surrounding liver
- **Delayed phase (3-5 minutes)** — continues washing out

This "**hyperenhancement in the arterial phase + washout later**" pattern is the **imaging diagnostic criterion** for HCC. **If it matches this pattern in a patient with HBV, you can diagnose HCC directly, no biopsy needed.**

---

## Contrast MRI: more sensitive, especially for small lesions

MRI advantages:

- **No radiation** (important, because HBV patients monitor for life)
- Better soft-tissue resolution
- Can use a **liver-specific contrast agent** (Gd-EOB-DTPA, brand name "Primovist")

**Primovist MRI is currently the most sensitive method for diagnosing HCC lesions < 2 cm.**

Primovist is taken up by normal liver cells but **not by HCC cells** — so in the delayed phase (20 minutes after injection), HCC shows up as a "black hole," very easy to spot.

**Recommended scenarios:**

- Small nodule found on ultrasound
- AFP rising but CT is negative
- Annual deep screening for high-risk patients

## Screening cadence summary

| Situation | US | CT/MRI | AFP | Frequency |
|------|------|--------|-----|------|
| Chronic HBV, F0-F2 | ✅ | As needed | ✅ | 6-12 months |
| Chronic HBV, F3 | ✅ | Yearly | ✅ | 6 months |
| Cirrhosis | ✅ | Yearly deep scan | ✅ | 3-4 months |
| Suspicious nodule on US | — | ✅ immediately | ✅ | Depends on result |
| Family history of liver cancer | ✅ | Yearly | ✅ | 3-6 months |

## How to use AFP correctly

Chapter 10 covered AFP's limitations. A few more points:

- AFP **can't be used alone** — 30-40% of HCCs don't raise it
- Rising AFP **doesn't always mean cancer** — active hepatitis or liver regeneration can raise it too
- **Trends matter more than single readings** — rapid short-term rise (say, doubling in 3 months), even at low absolute levels, needs attention
- **AFP-L3 + PIVKA-II** combo improves specificity — get them if available

---

## Intensified monitoring for high-risk groups

The following groups have higher HCC risk and should be screened every 3-4 months:

- **Cirrhotic patients** (regardless of DNA status)
- **Family history of HCC**
- **Men > 40 or women > 50 with chronic HBV**
- **HBsAg quantitation > 10⁴**
- **Coexisting fatty liver, diabetes, alcohol use**
- **Coexisting HDV or HCV**

## A real-life scenario

A 45-year-old man with hepatitis B, on TAF for 10 years, DNA undetectable long-term, ALT normal.

Last year an ultrasound picked up a 1.2 cm nodule in the right liver lobe. His doctor recommended **contrast MRI (Primovist)**.

Result: arterial-phase enhancement + delayed "black hole" — **classic HCC**.

Because the lesion was < 2 cm with no distant spread, the doctor did **radiofrequency ablation (RFA)** — a needle heats and destroys the tumor with minimal trauma.

Three years later, the patient is still on regular follow-up, no recurrence.

**The point of this story — early detection, early action. HBV-related HCC can absolutely be managed toward cure.**

And all of that rests on **regular imaging surveillance**.

---

## 📍 Key Points

- The core job of imaging is **early HCC detection**
- **Ultrasound every 3-6 months** is the baseline for all HBV patients
- **Contrast CT / MRI** for nodule characterization
- **Primovist MRI is the most sensitive diagnostic tool for small HCCs**
- HCC imaging criterion: **hyperenhancement in the arterial phase + washout later**
- AFP can't be used alone; trends matter more than single readings
- Early-detected HCC has 5-year survival > 70%
