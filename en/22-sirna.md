# Ch 22 · siRNA

> Turning off the HBsAg factory switch.

---

## siRNA in one minute

siRNA = **small interfering RNA**.

How it works:

1. You inject an **artificially designed double-stranded RNA**
2. This RNA sequence is **complementary to HBV mRNA**
3. It gets into the cell and binds the HBV mRNA
4. The cell's RNAi machinery **chops that mRNA in two** and degrades it
5. HBsAg and other HBV proteins **can't be made**

**One line: it makes HBV's mRNA "all void."**

---

## Why it's revolutionary

Go back to that table in Chapter 4. **siRNA hits mRNA, not DNA.**

Result:

- **mRNA from cccDNA** — cut
- **mRNA from integrated DNA** — **also cut**

**That's the "double-source coverage" that NUCs can't do.**

Clinical data shows siRNA can bring HBsAg down **2-3 logs**, from 10⁴ down to 10¹ range. That's a depth NUCs couldn't achieve in decades.

---

## Main candidates today

**Vir-2218** (Vir Biotechnology)
- Subcutaneous injection
- Once every 4 weeks
- Already in Phase II/III
- Often combined with Peg-IFN or the therapeutic antibody VIR-3434

**JNJ-3989** (Arrowhead / Janssen)
- Also called ARO-HBV
- Phase II/III
- Combined with NUCs

**RG6346** (Roche / Dicerna)
- Also called DCR-HBVS
- Phase II
- Data shows strong HBsAg reduction

**Elebsiran** (Alnylam)
- Phase II
- Long half-life, less frequent dosing

**Chinese candidates**: projects from Hengrui, Chia Tai Tianqing, Silence Therapeutics collaborations, and others. Specific phases should be cross-checked against the latest data.

---

## GalNAc delivery: getting drugs right to the liver

siRNA made it into the clinic thanks to one big technological breakthrough: **GalNAc conjugation**.

GalNAc (N-acetylgalactosamine) is a sugar specifically recognized by the **ASGPR receptor** on hepatocytes. Link siRNA to GalNAc, and after injection:

- Subcutaneous injection enters the bloodstream
- Blood flows through the liver, hepatocyte ASGPR "grabs" the GalNAc
- siRNA is internalized into the hepatocyte
- Reaches exactly where HBV mRNA lives

**That's why modern siRNA drugs almost all target the liver. GalNAc naturally only recognizes hepatocytes.**

---

## Effect: deep HBsAg reduction

Some representative clinical data:

**JNJ-3989 + NUC**:
- HBsAg drops **1.5-2.5 logs**
- Most patients reach < 100 IU/mL
- A few reach < 10 IU/mL

**VIR-2218 monotherapy**:
- HBsAg drops **1.5-1.7 logs**
- Some patients approach the threshold for functional cure

**siRNA + Peg-IFN**:
- Even deeper HBsAg reduction
- A few reach HBsAg loss (around 5-15%)

**siRNA + therapeutic vaccine or PD-1**:
- Early data shows additive effects
- Off-treatment maintenance rates still being observed

---

## Pros

- **Deep HBsAg reduction.** This is the core weapon
- **Covers both cccDNA + integration.** Two sources
- **Friendly dosing frequency.** Usually every 4-12 weeks
- **Compared to oral NUCs, offers possibility of "off-treatment maintenance"**
- **Side effects are generally mild.** Injection site reactions, occasional ALT elevation

---

## Cons and challenges

**① HBsAg rebounds after stopping**
- siRNA doesn't eliminate cccDNA
- After stopping, mRNA gets transcribed again
- HBsAg slowly comes back up
- **Unless** the immune system was "retrained" before stopping

**② Requires long-term or combination regimen**
- Monotherapy struggles to cure on its own
- Must be paired with an immune strategy

**③ Immune-rebound-related ALT elevation**
- Some patients see ALT rise during or after treatment
- Usually a sign of immune activation (can be good)
- But could also be over-activation (bad)
- **Close monitoring needed**

**④ Long-term safety still accumulating**
- siRNA has been in HBV clinical use less than 10 years
- Long-term (> 5 years) data still limited

## siRNA vs ASO, quick comparison

siRNA and ASO are often discussed together, but the mechanisms differ slightly:

| Dimension | siRNA | ASO |
|-----------|-------|-----|
| Chemical structure | Double-stranded RNA | Single-stranded DNA-like |
| Cutting mechanism | RISC complex cuts mRNA | RNase H cuts mRNA |
| Target selection | Stricter | Slightly more flexible |
| Dosing frequency | 4-12 weeks | Weekly (bepirovirsen) |
| Delivery | GalNAc | Partly GalNAc, partly naked |
| Representative drug | VIR-2218, JNJ-3989 | Bepirovirsen |

**Both attack at the mRNA level. Similar effect, different details.**

## A patient's view

If you're following siRNA trials:

**Common entry requirements:**
- HBsAg > 500 or > 1000 IU/mL
- Already stable on NUC therapy
- No cirrhosis or only mild fibrosis
- ALT < 2-3 × ULN

**During the trial:**
- Frequent blood draws
- Subcutaneous injection (usually around the belly)
- Long follow-up

**After:**
- Off-treatment observation
- HBsAg rebound is common — don't despair
- A small share achieve functional cure — that's you

---

## The 5-10 year picture

If I had to bet, siRNA is **one of the most likely new drug types to be approved in the next 5 years**.

**Possible scenarios:**

- **2027-2029** — Bepirovirsen (ASO) or VIR-2218 (siRNA) approved as an **add-on** to NUCs
- **2030+** — siRNA + immunity combination regimens approved
- **2035+** — siRNA joins first-line standard therapy, replacing or combining with NUCs

**siRNA won't cure HBV alone. But it's the first drug class to make a real breakthrough on HBsAg.**

---

## 📍 Key Points

- siRNA **degrades HBV mRNA**, so HBsAg can't be made
- **Covers both cccDNA and integrated DNA sources**
- Delivered by GalNAc, precisely to the liver
- HBsAg drops deeply, 2-3 logs (NUCs can't do this)
- Main candidates: **VIR-2218, JNJ-3989, RG6346, Elebsiran**
- Monotherapy can't cure, needs pairing with immune strategy
- **One of the most likely new drugs to get approved in the next 5 years**
