# Ch 17 · Interferon: an old drug in a new role

> The drug with the worst side effects is also the one with the best shot at "stop-and-cure."

---

## A paradox

NUCs: **small side effects, can't be stopped, extremely low cure rate**.
Interferon: **big side effects, can be stopped, a minority actually get cured**.

This contradiction is why interferon still holds a place in the HBV field.

---

## What is interferon

Interferon (IFN) is an immune signaling molecule the body already makes — cells release it when they sense a virus, alerting neighboring cells to enter "defensive mode."

**In HBV we use exogenous alpha interferon**, given by injection.

It works two ways:

1. **Direct antiviral** — activates the cell's antiviral pathways
2. **Immune modulation** — boosts T-cell and NK-cell recognition of HBV

The second point is what fundamentally sets it apart from NUCs — **interferon "gives the immune system a hand," it doesn't "fight for the immune system."**

---

## Two formulations

**Standard interferon (IFN-α)**
- Injected every day or every other day
- Side effects come thick and fast
- Rarely used now

**Pegylated interferon (Peg-IFN)**
- Injected once a week
- More stable blood levels
- Side effects relatively manageable
- The current standard regimen

Standard course: **Peg-IFN-α-2a, 180 μg subcutaneous injection, once a week, for 48 weeks**.

---

## Efficacy: a minority really do get "cured"

Peg-IFN efficacy data (48-week course, 6 months after stopping):

- **HBeAg seroconversion rate: about 30%**
- **Long-term DNA suppression: about 30–40%**
- **HBsAg seroclearance rate: about 3–10%** — **something NUCs can't do!**

Some patients, through the 48-week course, achieve **persistent HBsAg negativity + Anti-HBs appearance** after stopping, entering the functional cure state.

**This is interferon's unique value — it's the first, and currently the only, drug that can achieve stop-and-cure within a finite course.**

---

## Who's most likely to benefit

Not everyone is a good fit for interferon. **Features of high-response candidates:**

- Young (< 40)
- **HBeAg positive**
- **HBV genotype A or B** (B is common in southern China)
- **Elevated baseline ALT** (2–5 × ULN, suggests the immune system is already active)
- **Moderate baseline HBV DNA** (< 10⁸)
- **Baseline HBsAg quantitation < 25000 IU/mL**
- **Early response** (HBsAg already dropping clearly by week 12 or 24)

If you check **multiple boxes**, talk to your doctor — you might be an interferon candidate.

If you are:
- Elderly
- Cirrhotic (especially decompensated)
- Have severe psychiatric illness
- Thyroid dysfunction
- Autoimmune disease
- Serious hematologic disease

**Not a fit for interferon.**

---

## Side effects: the honest part

Interferon has **many, common** side effects. You need to be mentally prepared.

**Very common (>50%):**

- Flu-like symptoms (fever, chills, headache, muscle aches)
- Fatigue, weakness
- Decreased appetite, weight loss

**Common (10–50%):**

- Mood changes: depression, irritability
- Sleep disturbance
- Skin reactions (redness and swelling at the injection site)
- Hair loss
- Reduced blood cells (white cells, platelets)
- Thyroid dysfunction

**Rare but serious (< 5%):**

- Severe depression → suicidal thoughts (**must tell family to watch for this**)
- Retinopathy
- Interstitial pneumonia
- Severe cytopenias
- Autoimmune disease activation

---

## Managing side effects

Not white-knuckling it — actively managing:

**Flu-like symptoms:**
- Take a fever reducer (acetaminophen) before injection
- Inject at bedtime so the worst window is during sleep
- Drink plenty of water

**Fatigue:**
- Own it, don't push through
- Nap during the day, prioritize important tasks
- Give family and work a heads-up

**Mood:**
- **Tell family in advance: I might not seem like myself for a few months**
- Check in on your mental state regularly
- Any sign of depression, tell the doctor right away, antidepressants if needed

**Blood cells:**
- Check CBC every 2–4 weeks
- White cells < 1.0 or platelets < 50 means dose reduction or pause

**Thyroid:**
- TSH + FT4 every 3 months
- Follow endocrinologist advice if abnormal

## Interferon + NUC combination?

A natural question — since the two drug mechanisms complement each other, can we combine them?

**Answer: yes, but it depends.**

**Main study conclusions:**

- **Concurrent combination (48 weeks)** — HBsAg drops faster than either alone, but **no significant advantage in HBeAg seroconversion** after stopping
- **Sequential therapy** — NUC first to suppress virus, then add interferon — **some small studies show higher HBsAg seroclearance**
- **Switch therapy** — after years on NUCs, add or switch to interferon — some success cases

**These regimens don't have strong evidence for widespread use yet, and are mostly used in:**

- Long-term NUC patients with HBsAg quantitation < 1500 IU/mL
- Those wanting a shot at stopping
- Doctor and patient have thorough discussion, patient can handle the side effects

---

## Interferon's place in the new-drug era

With new drugs (siRNA, ASO) entering clinical use, interferon's status is being reassessed.

Several directions being explored:

**① Interferon + siRNA/ASO** — antigen pressure comes down first, then interferon activates the immune system — **one of the most promising combinations**

**② Interferon substitutes** — more precise TLR agonists, interferon-like effects with fewer side effects

**③ Interferon as standalone** — for certain patient subsets, single-agent interferon is still first-line (especially HBeAg-positive + high-response profile)

**Interferon isn't going away. It'll become part of combination therapy.**

---

## A real story

A 32-year-old woman, HBeAg positive, baseline HBsAg 12000 IU/mL, ALT 92 U/L, genotype B.

Her doctor assessed her as a high-response candidate and recommended 48 weeks of Peg-IFN.

The first 3 months were tough — one shot a week, fevered and exhausted the next day; her mood dipped, she and her husband argued several times; she lost 6 kg.

**But at week 24** — HBsAg quantitation had dropped from 12000 to 800, and HBeAg turned negative.

She finished the 48 weeks. Six months after stopping: HBsAg quantitation 42 (near the functional cure threshold).

Eighteen more months on, HBsAg went fully negative, Anti-HBs appeared, and she **achieved functional cure**.

**Stories like this aren't the norm. But they exist, and this book wants you to know that possibility is real.**

---

## 📍 Key Points

- Interferon **is antiviral + immune-modulating at once**, the only drug that can achieve "stop-and-cure within a finite course"
- Standard regimen: **Peg-IFN 48 weeks**
- HBsAg seroclearance rate about 3–10% — **much higher than NUCs**
- **High-response features**: young, HBeAg+, genotype A/B, high baseline ALT
- Many and obvious side effects, need **mental preparation + family support**
- In the new-drug era, interferon will become **part of combination therapy**
