# Ch 36 · How to Evaluate a "New Drug"

> Every year there are "major breakthrough" headlines. Most of them aren't.

---

## A basic framework

To evaluate a new drug, ask these questions:

**1. What exactly does it improve?**
- Virology (DNA, HBsAg)?
- Clinical endpoints (cirrhosis, HCC, death)?
- Quality of life (no more meds)?

**2. How much better than existing options?**
- 5%? 20%? 100%?
- Is there a control group?
- Is the difference clinically meaningful?

**3. Which patients benefit?**
- Specific populations?
- Any Precondition?

**4. What are the side effects?**
- Common vs serious
- Short-term vs long-term
- Manageable vs unmanageable

**5. When can it be used?**
- Already approved? In which countries?
- Still in Phase III? When does it read out?
- Insurance coverage?

---

## The three phases of clinical trials

**Phase I**
- Number of people: 20-100
- Goal: safety, dosing
- **Can't prove efficacy** — just proves "no major problems"

**Phase II**
- Number of people: 100-300
- Goal: preliminary efficacy, dose optimization
- **You can see signals, but can't make firm conclusions**

**Phase III**
- Number of people: 500-3000
- Goal: prove efficacy, used for registration
- **Gold standard, this is heavyweight evidence**

**When you read about a new drug, always check which phase it's in:**
- Phase I data = baby soldiers
- Phase II data = junior squad
- Phase III data = regular army

---

## Five red-flag signals

If you see these phrases, stay alert:

**🚩 "The results are encouraging"**
- Usually means "some trend but not significant"
- Or "effective in a subset"

**🚩 "Up to X%"**
- "Up to 30%" ≠ "30% for everyone"
- Usually the best subset

**🚩 "Approached significance"**
- Means p-value between 0.05 and 0.1
- That's **not significant**, don't dress it up

**🚩 "Expected approval next year"**
- Companies often report optimistic timelines
- Delays are the norm

**🚩 "Shown in animal models to..."**
- Animals ≠ humans
- 90% of animal-effective drugs fail in humans

---

## Five green-flag signals

If you see these, you can pay a bit more attention:

**✅ Primary endpoint is "sustained negative off-therapy"**
- Not "reduction during treatment"
- Shows the authors are chasing a real cure

**✅ Independent control + large sample**
- Phase III + placebo/standard-of-care control
- N > 500

**✅ Long follow-up (> 48 weeks)**
- Short-term rebound is the norm; sustained long-term is what counts

**✅ Consistent effect across multiple populations**
- Not just a hand-picked subgroup
- All genotypes, both sexes, all ages

**✅ Results are independently replicated**
- Multiple centers, multiple trials, similar results

## A scoring sheet

For any new drug news, fill this out:

| Dimension | Score (1-5) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Journal/source | ? | NEJM=5, conference abstract=3, company release=2 |
| Trial phase | ? | III=5, II=3, I=1 |
| Sample size | ? | >1000=5, 500=4, 100=2 |
| Control arm | ? | RCT=5, single-arm=1 |
| Meaning of primary endpoint | ? | Off-therapy durability=5, DNA=2 |
| Effect size | ? | 20%+ difference=5, 5% difference=1 |
| Long follow-up | ? | > 1 year=5, < 6 months=1 |
| Manageable side effects | ? | All mild=5, many severe=1 |

**Total:**
- 32+ = very likely a real major breakthrough
- 24-31 = worth watching
- 16-23 = early evidence, keep observing
- < 16 = cautious optimism

## A negative example

Suppose a company announces:

> "Our new drug XYZ shows 100% suppression of HBV DNA in mice. Clinical trials expected next year."

**5-minute evaluation:**

- Phase: **preclinical** (mice)
- Sample: How many mice? How many weeks?
- Endpoint: DNA suppression (**old drugs already do this in humans**)
- Long-term: mice don't have a chronic infection model
- Timing: "expected next year" = empty talk
- Side effects: mouse data doesn't extrapolate

**Conclusion: noise. File it away, no need to get excited.**

---

## A positive example

Suppose NEJM publishes:

> "Bepirovirsen 300 mg weekly, 24 weeks, in 500 chronic hepatitis B patients stable on nucleoside therapy, primary endpoint (sustained HBsAg negative 24 weeks off-therapy) reached 20% vs 1% control."

**5-minute evaluation:**

- Phase: **Phase III**
- Sample: 500 (enough)
- Endpoint: **off-therapy durability** (strong)
- Effect: **20% vs 1%, a 20-fold difference**
- Journal: NEJM (top-tier)
- Long-term: 24 weeks (medium, longer would be nice)

**Conclusion: heavyweight evidence, worth reading deeply, tracking, and discussing with your doctor.**

---

## How to track a drug's long-term performance

After a drug reaches Phase III, it still has to go through:

1. **Phase III primary endpoint readout** (first major data drop)
2. **Phase III extended follow-up** (long-term durability)
3. **Registration filing** (FDA/EMA/NMPA)
4. **Approval** (6-12 months)
5. **Launch**
6. **Insurance negotiation**
7. **Real-world data accumulation**

**New data can appear at each step. You should:**
- Remember key drug names (like Bepirovirsen, VIR-2218, etc. — see Ch 34 watchlist)
- Search for the latest papers every six months
- Watch for disclosures at company investor days

---

## Three mindsets to avoid

**① Over-optimism**
- One Phase II data doesn't mean approval
- One animal model doesn't mean cure
- One press release doesn't mean scientific evidence

**② Over-pessimism**
- Some failed programs don't mean the whole direction is dead
- One side-effect report doesn't mean the drug is toxic
- One commercial setback doesn't mean the science is broken

**③ Chasing the new anxiously**
- You don't need to check the news every day
- You don't need to join every trial
- **Every six months is enough**

---

## One mindset

**New drug progress is a marathon, not a sprint.**

What you need to do isn't sprint every day; it's **pace yourself, keep up, and don't fall behind**.

---

## 📍 Key Points

- 5 evaluation questions: **what improves, how much better, who benefits, side effects, when available**
- Three phases: **I (safety), II (signal), III (evidence)**
- 5 red flags: vague language, "up to X", "approached significance", optimistic timelines, animal data
- 5 green flags: **off-therapy endpoint, large controlled sample, long follow-up, multiple populations, independent replication**
- **8-dimension scoring sheet** to help you rate
- Mindset: **no ecstasy, no doom, no anxious chasing**
