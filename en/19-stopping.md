# Ch 19 · The science of stopping

> Who can stop, how to stop, and what happens after.

---

## One-line opening

**Stopping is the highest-risk and most underestimated decision in chronic HBV management.**

Every patient inwardly wants to stop. The doctor knows it, you know it. But **stopping haphazardly can trigger serious consequences** — from viral rebound to fulminant hepatitis, and in extreme cases, death.

This chapter is about: when you can stop, how to stop, and what to watch after stopping.

---

## Three stopping scenarios

**Scenario A. Reaching functional cure**
- HBsAg negative + DNA negative + normal ALT
- No long-term rebound after stopping
- **This is "ideal stopping"**

**Scenario B. Long-term suppression, want to try stopping**
- Years on NUCs, DNA long undetectable
- But HBsAg still positive
- **High risk, needs strict selection + close monitoring**

**Scenario C. Forced stopping**
- Severe adverse reactions
- Financial reasons
- Pregnancy or other medical needs
- **Need to switch or bridge, not bare stopping**

---

## AASLD/EASL/APASL consensus on stopping

**Stopping criteria for HBeAg-positive patients:**
- HBeAg seroconversion (HBeAg negative + Anti-HBe positive)
- DNA undetectable, sustained for **12+ months** (consolidation period)
- No cirrhosis

**Stopping criteria for HBeAg-negative patients:**
- A few guidelines allow stopping when strict criteria are met
- Most guidelines recommend **long-term treatment** — unless HBsAg seroclearance is reached

**Cirrhosis patients:**
- **Stopping absolutely not recommended** (even if functional cure is reached, extreme caution needed)

---

## What happens after stopping

Different patients react very differently. Understand these "typical patterns":

**Pattern 1. Completely silent** — the lucky few
- DNA stays undetectable after stopping
- HBsAg stable or dropping
- The best-case "functional cure preserved" scenario

**Pattern 2. Virological rebound** — **about 60–80%**
- 6–12 weeks after stopping, DNA gradually rises
- ALT may go up
- Need to **restart treatment**

**Pattern 3. Immune flare + seroclearance** — **a minority good outcome**
- After stopping, DNA rebounds and ALT rises sharply (immune system is fighting)
- Immune system "takes over" viral clearance
- Some patients eventually reach HBsAg seroclearance
- **Some new guidelines call this "NUC-cessation-induced immune response"**

**Pattern 4. Immune flare + liver injury** — **dangerous scenario**
- ALT rises sharply (> 10 × ULN)
- Albumin falls, bilirubin rises, PT prolonged
- A few progress to **liver failure**
- Must restart treatment immediately, severe cases need hospitalization/transplant

**Pattern 5. HBsAg seroclearance = functional cure** — **best outcome**
- HBsAg fully disappears during or after stopping
- Anti-HBs appears
- Very rare, but does happen

## Who's most likely to "stop successfully"

**Features of stopping candidates:**

- **Young** (< 50)
- No cirrhosis
- **HBsAg quantitation < 100 IU/mL** (strong predictor)
- **HBV RNA undetectable**
- **HBcrAg very low**
- No family history of HCC
- Able to monitor closely

**HBsAg quantitation < 100 IU/mL is the most important predictor.** Some studies show that in these patients after stopping:

- HBsAg seroclearance rate as high as 20–40%
- But about 30–40% still need to restart treatment

---

## How to stop: practical workflow

**A. 3 months before deciding:**
- Full assessment: DNA, HBsAg quantitation, HBV RNA, HBcrAg, ALT, fibrosis, imaging
- Mental prep: accept the possibility of needing to restart treatment
- Signed doctor document recording the risk discussion
- **Notify family** — during rebound/flare, someone needs to watch you

**B. How to stop:**
- **Cold stop** — stop directly, no taper. NUCs have no withdrawal syndrome.
- **Gradual taper** — some researchers try half-dose then stop, evidence is limited
- **Generally, direct stop**

**C. Post-stopping monitoring:**

| Time | Monitoring | Frequency |
|------|------------|-----------|
| First 3 months | DNA + ALT + HBsAg | **Every 4 weeks** |
| 3–6 months | Same + HBeAg + Anti-HBe | Every 4–6 weeks |
| 6–12 months | Full panel + AFP + ultrasound | Every 8–12 weeks |
| After 12 months | Standard monitoring | Every 3 months |

**Key warning thresholds:**

- **ALT > 5 × ULN** — alert, contact your doctor
- **ALT > 10 × ULN + rising bilirubin + prolonged PT** — **emergency, immediately restart treatment + hospital evaluation**
- **DNA > 10⁴ and rising** — usually a restart is recommended

---

## The philosophy of when to restart

If DNA rebounds after stopping, must you restart immediately?

**Not necessarily.**

Some doctors wait 12–24 weeks to see **whether the immune system takes over** — if ALT rises but DNA starts dropping, immunity is doing its job.

- ALT rising + DNA falling → **observe**, seroconversion possible
- ALT rising + DNA rising → **restart treatment**
- Any sign of liver failure → **immediate restart + hospital**

**This judgment call needs an experienced hepatologist. Don't decide on your own without professional guidance.**

---

## Pregnancy scenarios

Stopping in pregnancy is another complex case:

- **High-viral-load mothers (DNA > 2 × 10⁵ IU/mL)** start **TDF** in late pregnancy (weeks 24–28) to block mother-to-child transmission
- Postpartum stopping is an option, but with close monitoring
- **Breastfeeding** on TDF is relatively safe
- ALT may rise after stopping — monitoring needed

---

## Psychological angle

Patients who want to stop often have this feeling: **"I want to break free from this disease."**

That's completely understandable. But remember:

- Stopping isn't "victory" — **it's a risk management decision**
- A failed attempt doesn't mean you can never stop — **new drugs may give you a new chance**
- Long-term NUC therapy is not itself a failure — it's protected your liver

**If you can't stop this time, don't be discouraged. Wait for new drugs, wait for a better shot.**

---

## 📍 Key Points

- Stopping is the **highest-risk decision** in chronic HBV, don't do it on your own
- Three scenarios: functional cure, long-term suppression wanting to stop, forced stopping
- Rebound rate after stopping is 60–80%, a minority can reach HBsAg seroclearance
- **HBsAg quantitation < 100 IU/mL is the strongest predictor**
- Strict monitoring after stopping, especially **every 4–6 weeks for the first 6 months**
- Key warning: **ALT > 10×ULN + rising bilirubin + prolonged PT = emergency**
- Cirrhosis patients: **stopping not recommended**
