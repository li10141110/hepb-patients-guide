# Ch 1 · It Started with a Drop of Blood

> One drop of blood, one Nobel Prize, and a 60-year question that still isn't finished.

---

## An accidental discovery

In 1963, an American doctor named Baruch Blumberg was doing something nobody thought was interesting — comparing serum samples from people around the world. What he wanted to know was, "Why do some people get sick more easily than others?"

Then, in a blood sample from an Australian aboriginal person, he found a strange antigen. It reacted with an antibody in the serum of an American hemophilia patient. Blumberg gave it a plain name: **Australia antigen (Au)**.

Over the next few years, the puzzle started coming together:

- The antigen was unusually common in leukemia patients
- Down syndrome patients were positive at high rates too
- Healthy people who got blood transfusions containing it developed **acute hepatitis**

By 1967, the answer was clear — this "Australia antigen" was **the surface protein of the hepatitis B virus**. Today we call it **HBsAg**.

In 1976, Blumberg won the Nobel Prize.

That one drop of blood was the starting line of a half-century tug-of-war between humans and HBV.

---

## Before that, we'd seen it but never looked at it

People had seen this disease long before Blumberg.

- In the 5th century BC, Hippocrates wrote about "epidemic jaundice"
- In the 19th century, some people in Europe got hepatitis after smallpox vaccination, and nobody knew why
- During WWII, a US Army yellow fever vaccine containing human serum infected 50,000 soldiers — back then it was called **serum hepatitis**
- In 1947, British doctor MacCallum split hepatitis into type A (oral) and type B (blood)

"Hepatitis B" was just an epidemiology label. **Nobody had actually seen the virus.**

The point of Blumberg's drop of blood: for the first time, that invisible enemy got pulled under a microscope.

---

## Seeing the virus

Once the antigen was found, the race was on.

- **1970** — British scientist David Dane saw the whole virus with an electron microscope, 42 nanometers across. Textbooks still call it a **Dane particle**.
- **1979** — HBV genome sequenced. Just 3,200 bases. One of the smallest animal DNA viruses.
- **1986** — Recombinant DNA vaccine goes on the market. **The first genetically engineered vaccine in history.**

From "invisible jaundice" to "a virus you can see" to "a disease you can prevent" — 20 years.

---

## So why write this book

Someone will ask: we have a vaccine, so what's left to talk about?

Here's the thing — **a vaccine protects the future. It doesn't fix the past.**

The generation born before the vaccine got widespread — 250 million chronic carriers globally. They're still taking pills every day, worrying about rebound if they stop, worrying about cirrhosis, worrying about liver cancer.

And right now, we're in a strange window:

1. The old drugs can suppress the virus but can't clear it
2. A new generation of drugs (siRNA, ASO, therapeutic vaccines) is in phase 3, and **functional cure** is a target we can actually talk about for the first time
3. CRISPR is starting to aim at cccDNA — "complete cure" isn't science fiction anymore

**The past 60 years were the era of control. The next 10 might be the era of cure.**

If you're a patient, or someone close to you is — what's about to happen this decade is worth watching with your own eyes.

## 📍 Key Points

- 1963, Blumberg discovers HBsAg, kicks off the HBV story
- 1970 the virus is seen, 1979 sequenced, 1986 vaccine launched
- The vaccine protects the future, but existing patients need a cure
- 2020-2030 is the critical window from "control" to "cure"
