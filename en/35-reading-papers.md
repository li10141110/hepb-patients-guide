# Ch 35 · How a Patient Reads Papers

> I'm not a medical professional, but I've read hundreds of HBV papers. This chapter is about how I do it.

---

## A premise

You don't need to become an expert. You just need to **understand the key 20% of the content** — because the other 80% of technical detail doesn't affect your decisions.

**Goal:** Be able to figure out what a paper says, whether it's reliable, and whether it's relevant to you.

---

## Three main sources

**1. PubMed** — the central repository of medical papers
- https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov
- Free
- Keyword searches: "HBsAg loss", "HBV cure", "siRNA hepatitis B"

**2. ClinicalTrials.gov** — the clinical trial registry
- https://clinicaltrials.gov
- Free
- Lets you check: which drugs are in trials, recruitment status, readout timing

**3. bioRxiv / medRxiv** — preprints (not yet peer-reviewed)
- https://www.biorxiv.org / https://www.medrxiv.org
- Free
- Newest and fastest, but **not peer-reviewed** — read with caution

**Secondary sources:**

- **Society guidelines**: AASLD, EASL, APASL, and the Chinese Society of Hepatology
- **Conference abstracts**: AASLD and EASL annual meetings (usually open on the journal websites)
- **Company investor materials**: drug maker IR pages (freshest data disclosures)

---

## The structure of a paper

Most medical papers follow this format:

1. **Abstract** — 200-300 word summary
2. **Introduction** — background
3. **Methods** — how it was done
4. **Results** — what was found
5. **Discussion** — what it means, limitations
6. **References**

**80% of the information is in the Abstract. Read the Abstract first.**

---

## The 5-minute version of reading a paper

**Step 1 (30 seconds): Title + journal**

- Are the keywords in the title?
- Is it a high-impact journal?
- Is it a direction I care about?

**Step 2 (2 minutes): Abstract**

Look for 4 key points:
- **What drug was studied**
- **Who the population is**
- **What the primary endpoint is**
- **What the result is**

**Step 3 (1 minute): Figures and tables**

The key conclusions of a medical paper are usually in:
- **Figure 1** — patient flow diagram or mechanism of action
- **Figure 2-3** — main results data
- **Table 1** — baseline characteristics

**Step 4 (1 minute): Conclusion**

How do the authors themselves describe it? Conservative or aggressive?

**Step 5 (30 seconds): Limitations**

- How big is the sample? (< 50 = early stage)
- How long is the follow-up? (< 24 weeks = short)
- Is there a control group?

## 5 questions to judge reliability

**Q1: Journal quality?**

Top-tier (must-read):
- NEJM, Lancet, JAMA, Nature, Science, Cell

Hepatology specialty (must-read):
- Journal of Hepatology, Hepatology, Gut

Mid-tier:
- J Viral Hepatitis, Liver International, Aliment Pharmacol Ther

**Journal quality isn't the only criterion, but it's a good starting point.**

**Q2: Sample size?**

- < 10: lab or small case series
- 10-50: early clinical
- 50-200: Phase II
- 200-1000: Phase III
- \> 1000: large-scale

**The bigger the sample, the more reliable.**

**Q3: Is there a control group?**

- **RCT (randomized controlled trial)** — gold standard
- **Single-arm study** — no control, weak evidence
- **Observational study** — post-hoc analysis, possible bias

**Q4: What's the primary endpoint?**

The key is whether the **primary endpoint** has clinical meaning:
- ✅ HBsAg negative 24 weeks after stopping treatment = strong
- ⚠️ HBsAg drop at end of treatment = medium
- ❌ Only HBV DNA drop = weak

**Q5: Who funded it?**

- Government / national grants → relatively independent
- Drug company → possibly biased, but the data itself still needs to be examined

**It's not that drug-company-funded work should be ignored, it just means you should **keep an extra eye out**.**

## Quickly judging "is this relevant to me"

Look at two things:

**① The study population**

- Am I HBeAg+ or -?
- Am I genotype B/C?
- What range is my HBsAg quantitation in?
- What's my age and gender?

Check the enrollment criteria. If they're **completely different from you**, the conclusions may not apply.

**② The primary endpoint**

What do I care about?
- Off-therapy durability? Check if that's the primary endpoint
- HBsAg seroclearance? Check the primary endpoint
- Side effects? Check the safety data

**"This paper shows 20% of patients achieved HBsAg seroclearance" — but you might not be in that 20%.**

## A real example

Suppose you find: *"Bepirovirsen in chronic hepatitis B: 24-week results of a phase 3 trial"*, NEJM 2026.

**The 5-minute read:**

1. **Title** — Bepirovirsen phase 3 results, in NEJM. **Must-read**.
2. **Abstract** —
   - Drug: Bepirovirsen 300 mg weekly
   - Population: adults stable on nucleoside therapy, HBsAg > 100
   - Primary endpoint: sustained HBsAg negative + DNA negative 24 weeks off-therapy
   - Result: **20% met the primary endpoint** (treatment group) vs 1% (control)
3. **Figure 2** — HBsAg decline curve over time
4. **Conclusion** — significantly better than control, planning to file for registration
5. **Limitations** — proportion of Asian patients, long-term (> 1 year) data still awaited

**After 5 minutes, you can answer:**
- Bepirovirsen lets 1 in 5 patients reach functional cure
- But if your HBsAg > 3000, the data may be less applicable to you
- Longer follow-up data is still needed

## What to do when you don't understand something

**Don't understand a technical term?**
- Google: term + "intro" or "explain"
- Or ask ChatGPT/Claude: "explain XX in simple language"

**Don't understand the statistics?**
- Odds Ratio (OR) — relative risk, >1 = worse, <1 = better
- Hazard Ratio (HR) — similar
- P value — smaller is more significant, < 0.05 is generally considered "meaningful"
- 95% CI — confidence interval, non-overlapping means the difference is real

**You don't have to become a statistician. Just be wary of "borderline significance" claims when you read the conclusions.**

---

## A suggestion: build a "one paper per week" habit

Pick a direction you're interested in (say, siRNA) and read one paper for 5 minutes each week.

Six months in, you'll:
- Know the main players in the field
- Be comfortable with the core terms
- Keep up with the main progress
- Be able to have deeper conversations with your doctor

**This is how I got started.**

---

## 📍 Key Points

- Three main sources: PubMed, ClinicalTrials.gov, bioRxiv
- 5-minute paper read: title → Abstract → figures → Conclusion → limitations
- 5 reliability questions: **journal, sample size, control, endpoint, funding**
- Judge relevance to you: **look at the population + primary endpoint**
- When stuck: **Google + AI to help explain**
- **One paper a week, and in six months you're a pro**
