# Ch 5 · Global and Chinese Epidemiology

> Numbers are cold. But every number is a real person.

> ⚠️ Data in this chapter still needs to be cross-checked against the latest WHO reports and the Polaris Observatory dataset.

---

## Let's build an intuition first

Chronic HBV carriers globally, roughly —

> **250 million people.**

What does that mean?

- Roughly the population of **Brazil**
- **6 times** the number of HIV-infected people
- **5 times** the number of chronic HCV cases

**Chronic hepatitis B is the most common chronic viral infection in the world. Period.**

Every year, HBV-related disease kills around **800,000 to 1.1 million** people worldwide — **on par with AIDS, more than malaria**.

---

## An uneven world map

WHO splits prevalence into three tiers:

| Tier | HBsAg positive rate | Representative regions |
|------|-------------|---------|
| High | ≥ 8% | Sub-Saharan Africa, Pacific Islands |
| Medium | 2-7% | Eastern Europe, Mediterranean, South Asia, Central/South America |
| Low | < 2% | Western Europe, North America, Japan/Korea/Australia |

Some representative numbers (recent estimates):

- **China** ~5.6% (was 9.75% in 1992)
- **Vietnam** ~8%
- **Mongolia** ~9% (one of the world's highest)
- **India** 1-3%
- **US** ~0.3%
- **Canada** ~0.4%
- **Japan** ~0.6%

**Over half of the world's HBV patients live in the Western Pacific region** — mainly China, Vietnam, the Philippines.

---

## China: from 9.75% to 5.6%

China is **the most notable case study** in global HBV control.

Timeline:

- **1979** — first survey, positive rate 8.83%
- **1992** — second survey, **9.75%** — WHO labels it a high-prevalence region
- **1992** — HBV vaccine added to childhood immunization (self-paid)
- **2002** — added to the national immunization program (free)
- **2005** — newborn vaccine fully free
- **2006** — under-5 rate down to **0.96%**
- **2014** — under-5s at **0.32%**
- **2020** — general population estimated at **5.6%**

In one line:

> **China's HBV vaccination program may be one of the most successful public health interventions of the past 40 years.**

From 1992 to today, an estimated **30 million chronic infections have been prevented**.

**But the stock problem is still huge.**

At 5.6%, China still has **around 70 million chronic carriers**, close to a third of the global total.

Of those:
- About 20-30 million need antiviral treatment
- **Only 15-20% are actually on proper treatment**
- Huge numbers **don't know they're infected**

## Canada and the US: immigration-driven HBV

North America has low overall prevalence (< 0.5%), but huge internal variation.

**United States**:
- ~2.2-2.4 million infected in total
- **60%+ are foreign-born immigrants**
- Asian Americans have an HBsAg positive rate around 3-4% — **10x higher than white Americans**
- Undiagnosed rate as high as **65%**

**Canada**:
- ~250-300k infected total
- Mostly new immigrants
- Chinese, Vietnamese, and African-origin communities have significantly higher rates than the local average

For overseas Chinese, there's an awkward reality:

> **In China you were treated as an ordinary carrier. Move to North America, and your doctor may have no idea how to manage your HBV properly.**

That's part of what this book tries to fill in — bringing Chinese clinical experience, international guidelines, and new drug developments **into the same conversation**.

## Transmission routes: today isn't like the past

Three classic routes:

1. **Mother-to-child transmission** — historically the main route in China
2. **Blood exposure** — transfusions, needles, tattoos, shared razors
3. **Sexual contact**

Before the vaccine era, East Asia was dominated by **neonatal infection**, while Africa was dominated by **horizontal transmission in early childhood**.

**Why does age at infection determine chronicity risk?**

Because the immune response depends on how mature the immune system is:

| Age at infection | Chronicity rate |
|---------|-----------|
| Newborns | **~90%** |
| 1-5 years | 25-50% |
| Children over 5 | 5-10% |
| Adults | **< 5%** |

**That's why "newborn vaccine + immunoglobulin" can almost fully block mother-to-child transmission — it shuts the most dangerous window.**

## The vaccine: the first vaccine that could prevent cancer

The HBV vaccine has an impressive claim to fame:

> **The first vaccine in human history that could prevent cancer.**

Because what it prevents isn't just infection — it also prevents HBV-driven liver cancer.

A classic Taiwan study: after universal vaccination started in 1984, liver cancer rates in 6-19-year-olds dropped **70%** two decades later.

Three generations of vaccines:

- **1st gen** (1981) — plasma-derived
- **2nd gen** (1986 to today) — recombinant yeast, the global standard
- **3rd gen** — CpG adjuvant (Heplisav-B), approved in the US in 2017, only 2 shots

Standard schedule: **0, 1, 6 months, three doses**; newborns get an extra dose within 24 hours of birth.

After three doses, **90-95%** of people produce adequate antibodies (anti-HBs ≥ 10 mIU/mL).

A minority don't produce antibodies (**non-responders**), reasons include:
- Genetics (HLA type)
- Immunosuppression
- Older age, obesity, diabetes
- **Mother infected but blocking failed** (in which case treat as already infected, not as vaccine failure)

## Can the WHO 2030 target be met?

WHO's target:

> **By 2030, reduce new HBV infections by 90% and HBV-related deaths by 65%.**

Progress:

- **New infections** — close to on-track (thanks to the vaccine)
- **HBV-related deaths** — **behind target**, actually slowly rising

Reasons:
- 250 million stock; even with zero new infections, it takes decades to work through
- Global treatment coverage is only **~13%**
- Once liver cancer develops, prognosis is poor

To actually hit the WHO target, three things need to happen together:
1. Keep expanding vaccine coverage (especially African children)
2. Improve screening (globally 50% of infected people don't know)
3. **A breakthrough in curative therapy** (the battle for the next 10 years)

## Some numbers that made me stop and think

- Every **30 seconds**, someone in the world dies from HBV-related disease
- ~900,000 new liver cancer cases each year globally; **more than half are HBV-related**
- HBV-related deaths in China: ~350,000-400,000 per year
- Of infected people globally, **fewer than 1 in 7 gets proper treatment**
- Fewer than **40%** of infected people worldwide **know they're infected**

The numbers are cold, but they remind us:

**This is not a rare disease. This is a quiet, global, chronic public health crisis.**

---

## 📍 Key Points

- ~**250 million** chronic HBV carriers globally, more than HIV and HCV combined
- 800,000-1.1 million deaths per year, on par with HIV
- China went from 9.75% in 1992 to 5.6%, but stock is still around **70 million**
- Neonatal chronicity rate is 90%, adult infection < 5%
- Post-vaccine era: **stock >> new**
- North America is low-prevalence, but **immigrant communities have significantly higher rates**
- WHO 2030 progress: on track for new infections, behind on deaths
