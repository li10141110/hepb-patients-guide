# Ch 8 · Where HBsAg Comes From

> Get this, and you'll see why new drugs can do what old drugs can't.

---

## A simple question that gets overlooked

The doctor tells you: "HBsAg seroconversion means cure."

Then the question is — **where does HBsAg actually get made?**

The answer isn't one place. It's **two**:

1. **cccDNA** — the classic source
2. **Integrated DNA** — the long-overlooked source

The ratio between these two shifts across different stages of infection.

---

## Early infection: mainly from cccDNA

Freshly infected, or untreated chronic patients:

- cccDNA is active, pgRNA gets transcribed heavily
- HBsAg mainly comes from cccDNA transcription → S mRNA → surface protein
- HBV DNA, HBeAg, HBsAg often **move in the same direction**

This is the textbook "standard" picture of hep B.

---

## After long-term treatment: mainly from integrated DNA

After years on nucleoside drugs, the picture changes.

- HBV DNA is pushed below detection
- cccDNA count drops, and much of it gets "silenced"
- But you'll notice — **HBsAg is still positive, and stubbornly refuses to drop**

The Johns Hopkins team's 2025 study directly measured this phenomenon:

> **After long-term treatment, HBsAg transcription shifts from cccDNA to integrated DNA.**

In other words, **once cccDNA is suppressed, integrated DNA takes over HBsAg production.**

**This is why nucleoside drugs never achieve HBsAg seroconversion — they never targeted integrated DNA.**

---

## One picture makes it clear


---

## Why this "source shift" matters so much

Three reasons:

**① It explains the ceiling for old drugs**

Nucleoside drugs, interferon — all act on cccDNA-related steps. Once HBsAg mainly comes from integrated DNA, these drugs can't reach it.

**② It explains why new drugs can break through**

siRNA and ASO target **mRNA**. Integrated DNA also has to transcribe mRNA, so siRNA/ASO **can hit both**.

That last row in the Chapter 4 comparison table: "covers cccDNA + integrated" — that's what it means.

**③ It redefines what "HBsAg disappearance" means**

If HBsAg turns negative, it doesn't mean cccDNA is cleared, and doesn't mean integrated DNA is gone — **it just means both sources are quiet enough**.

That's why the term "functional cure" is so precise — **"functionally not producing antigen anymore," not "virus totally eradicated."**

---

## What about HBeAg

Let's also sort out HBeAg while we're at it, so it doesn't get confused.

- **HBeAg can only come from cccDNA.** Integrated DNA **doesn't produce HBeAg** (because integration sites usually lack a complete C region promoter).
- So HBeAg is a **direct indicator of how active cccDNA is**
- HBeAg turning negative usually means cccDNA transcription is heavily suppressed
- But HBsAg can still be positive — because integrated DNA is still churning it out

**That explains the molecular basis for the huge category of "HBeAg negative, HBsAg positive" patients.**

---

## What is HBV RNA

A new marker showing up frequently in recent clinical trials: **serum HBV RNA**.

What is it?

- cccDNA transcribes pgRNA. Most of it gets packaged into nucleocapsids
- A small fraction of pgRNA-packaged nucleocapsids gets secreted into the blood
- HBV RNA in the blood **mainly reflects active cccDNA transcription**

Why does it matter?

- HBV DNA below detection under nucleoside drugs doesn't mean cccDNA is inactive
- But if **HBV RNA is also undetectable**, cccDNA transcription itself is suppressed
- HBV RNA changes after stopping the drug can predict rebound risk

**HBV RNA is now used as a surrogate marker for cccDNA activity**, especially in new drug trials.

---

## What is HBcrAg

Another new marker: **Hepatitis B core-related antigen (HBcrAg)**.

It's a "mixture," containing:
- HBcAg (core antigen)
- HBeAg (e antigen)
- Some core protein precursors

Meaning: reflects the **total activity** of the cccDNA pool.

High HBcrAg suggests cccDNA is active. Low suggests it's suppressed or reduced.

**Clinically, HBcrAg is increasingly used to judge:**
- Post-treatment rebound risk
- Long-term HCC risk
- Deep antiviral effect of new drugs

---

## A combined marker table

| Marker | Reflects | Use |
|------|------|------|
| HBV DNA | Viral replication | Routine monitoring |
| HBsAg quantitative | cccDNA + integrated | Cure assessment |
| HBeAg | cccDNA activity | Staging, efficacy |
| **HBV RNA** | cccDNA transcription | **Surrogate endpoint in new drug trials** |
| **HBcrAg** | Total cccDNA pool | **Off-treatment prediction, HCC risk** |
| ALT/AST | Liver damage | Inflammation monitoring |

The first three are old friends. **The last two are key markers in the new drug era.**

---

## Back to cure: both sources need to be dealt with

One-line summary:

> **For HBsAg seroconversion, both cccDNA and integrated DNA have to be suppressed.**
>
> **For real eradication, cccDNA has to be cleared, infected cells have to die, and integrated DNA has to be silenced.**

**The entire logic of new combination therapies is built on this "two source" recognition.**

---

## 📍 Key Points

- HBsAg has two sources: cccDNA transcription + integrated DNA transcription
- Early infection is mainly cccDNA
- **After long-term treatment it's mainly integrated DNA** — that's the ceiling for nucleoside drugs
- siRNA / ASO hit mRNA, so they cover both sources
- HBeAg only comes from cccDNA, a direct marker of cccDNA activity
- **HBV RNA and HBcrAg are the next-generation surrogate markers for cccDNA activity**
