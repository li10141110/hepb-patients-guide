# Ch 15 · Nucleos(t)ide Analogs

> The absolute workhorse of the past 20 years, and still the foundation of every treatment plan today.

---

## The story of three letters

Ask a HBV patient what they take today, and the answer basically comes down to three:

**ETV · TDF · TAF**

They all belong to the **nucleos(t)ide analogs** family (NUCs, Nucleos(t)ide Analogs).

The principle: **impersonate natural nucleosides so the virus's reverse transcriptase mistakenly inserts them into new DNA, and the chain fails to extend.**

Step 5 of the life cycle chart in Chapter 2 is exactly where they get stuck.

---

## A short history: from lamivudine to TAF

**First generation: Lamivudine (LAM)**

- Approved in 1998
- The first oral HBV antiviral
- Short-term effective, but **resistance rate is extremely high** — > 70% at 5 years
- Basically not used today

**Second generation: Adefovir (ADV)**

- Approved in 2002
- Effective against lamivudine-resistant virus
- But **kidney toxicity** is obvious
- Has been replaced

**Third generation: Telbivudine (LdT)**

- Approved in 2006
- Reasonably potent antiviral effect
- But also **high resistance**
- Has faded out

**Fourth generation (today's workhorses): entecavir, TDF, TAF**

These three are **all first-line regimens today**.

---

## Entecavir (ETV)

**Baraclude**

- Launched in 2005
- Strong viral suppression
- **High barrier to resistance** — in treatment-naive patients, 5-year resistance rate < 1% (except in those who used lamivudine)
- Oral, once daily, on an empty stomach

**Dosing:**

- 0.5 mg/day — treatment-naive
- 1.0 mg/day — after lamivudine resistance

**Pros:**
- Strong potency
- Low resistance
- Plenty of long-term data
- Generics available, cheap

**Cons:**
- Lamivudine-resistant patients need a higher dose, and it works less well
- Empty-stomach dosing means strict timing
- Pregnancy data isn't as good as TDF's

---

## Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF)

**Viread**

- Approved for HBV in 2008
- Originally an HIV drug, later extended to HBV
- Potent, **extremely low resistance**
- Many generics, super cheap

**Dosing:** 300 mg/day

**Pros:**
- Strong potency
- **Almost no resistance reported over 10 years**
- Pregnancy category B (relatively safe)
- Cheap

**Cons:**
- **Kidney toxicity** — long-term use can damage the renal tubules
- **Bone density loss** — bone density monitoring is needed with long-term use
- **Use with caution in older adults and those with impaired kidney function**

---

## Tenofovir alafenamide fumarate (TAF)

**Vemlidy**

- Launched in 2016 (HBV indication)
- An improved **prodrug** version of TDF
- **Activated inside liver cells**, plasma concentration is only 10% of TDF's
- So **kidney and bone side effects are much reduced**

**Dosing:** 25 mg/day

**Pros:**
- Antiviral potency equal to TDF
- **Kidney-friendly**
- **Bone-friendly**
- One pill a day, no strict empty-stomach rule

**Cons:**
- **Expensive** (still patent-protected, brand price is much higher than TDF)
- **Metabolic side effects** — some patients see blood lipids go up
- Long-term data (10+ years) is still being built up

---

## How to choose between the three

**Prefer TAF when:**
- Age > 60
- Osteoporosis or low bone density
- Chronic kidney disease or diabetic nephropathy
- Long treatment plan (> 10 years)

**Prefer TDF when:**
- Financial pressure, need a cheap option
- Pregnancy
- Young patient with no kidney/bone issues
- Already need to control high cholesterol anyway

**Prefer ETV when:**
- No history of lamivudine resistance
- Want to avoid the kidney/bone effects of the nucleotide class
- Generics available, low price
- Willing to accept empty-stomach dosing

**The most common combinations for Chinese patients (after 2020):**
- New, treatment-naive young patient: **TAF** or **generic TDF**
- Older or under financial pressure: **generic ETV**

## Monitoring side effects

**Shared feature: generally very mild** — most patients have no noticeable discomfort on long-term therapy.

**Watch with TDF:**

- **Kidney function** — check serum creatinine, eGFR, and urine protein every 6–12 months
- **Bone density** — long-term users are advised to get a DEXA scan every 2 years
- Catch problems early, and you can switch to TAF

**Watch with TAF:**

- **Blood lipids** — yearly lipid panel
- Some patients need lifestyle or statin intervention for elevated LDL
- **Metabolic syndrome risk** — especially in patients with obesity, diabetes, or fatty liver

**Watch with ETV:**

- Relatively few
- Compliance with empty-stomach dosing
- Very rare lactic acidosis (high risk: severe cirrhosis)

## A few practical questions about dosing

**Q1: What if I miss a dose?**

- Within 12 hours: take it right away
- More than 12 hours: skip it, take the next dose on time
- **Do not double up**

**Q2: Can I stop when I have a cold?**

- **No. A cold doesn't affect NUCs, keep taking them on schedule.**

**Q3: Can I take them with other medications?**

- ETV: mind the empty-stomach window (no food/other meds 2 hours before or after)
- TDF/TAF: relatively flexible
- **Watch out for co-administered nephrotoxic drugs** (e.g., certain antibiotics, long-term NSAIDs)

**Q4: What about business trips and travel?**

- Carry a month's supply with you
- Time zone shift < 6 hours: no adjustment needed
- Time zone shift > 6 hours: check with your doctor in advance about adjusting timing

**Q5: Can I stop the medication on my own?**

- **Absolutely not.**
- Sudden discontinuation can trigger a **virological rebound + immune flare**, and in severe cases, liver failure
- Any stopping must be doctor-guided and closely monitored

---

## Handling resistance

Modern first-line drugs (ETV/TDF/TAF) have extremely low resistance rates. But if:

- **DNA keeps rising** (>1 log rebound)
- **Compliance is good** (ruling out missed doses)
- **History of lamivudine or adefovir use**

then resistance is possible. Handling:

- **Test resistance sites**
- **Switch or combine** (e.g., TDF + ETV combination)
- In modern practice, switching to **TDF or TAF** alone usually covers most resistance mutations

---

## The philosophy of long-term therapy

NUCs are "**long-term suppression**" drugs, not "**short-term cure**" drugs.

Most patients need to take them **long-term (years to lifelong)**.

- **Good news: side effects are generally mild, safety is high**
- **Bad news: HBsAg seroclearance rate is < 1% per year — clearing the virus is basically not on the table**

**The point of new drugs is to break this "lifelong dependence" status quo.**

Until new drugs enter routine use, **NUCs remain the foundation of every treatment plan** — including future combination therapies, which almost always use NUCs as the base.

---

## 📍 Key Points

- The three main nucleoside analogs: **ETV, TDF, TAF**
- All act at the **reverse transcription** step, suppressing new virus production
- **TAF is the improved version of TDF**, with fewer kidney/bone side effects but higher cost
- Resistance to first-line drugs is extremely low (< 1%), no need to worry about it too much
- Main side effects: TDF kidney/bone, TAF lipids, ETV the fewest overall
- **Don't stop on your own** — high rebound risk
- NUCs are "suppression," not "cure" — but still the foundation of every regimen
