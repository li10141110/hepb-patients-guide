# Ch 6 · Immune Tolerance and T Cell Exhaustion

> The virus doesn't "beat" the immune system. It lulls it to sleep.

---

## A counterintuitive fact

Most people who get infected clear HBV on their own.

- Adults with acute infection: **over 95% clear it themselves**
- Chronic cases are mostly people infected **as newborns or young children** — because their immune systems weren't fully developed yet

In other words, **the problem isn't a weak immune system. It's that the immune system "didn't realize there was an enemy" at the critical moment.**

This is called **immune tolerance**.

---

## Where does immune tolerance come from

A newborn's immune system isn't mature yet. If HBV gets in at that point, the immune system mistakes it for **"one of us"** and doesn't launch a clearance response.

Once that "one of us" judgment is locked in, it's very hard to reverse.

Even decades later, when the immune system has matured, it stays in **pretend-sleep mode** toward HBV.

That's why chronic HBV patients can **coexist with the virus for decades** — not because the immune system can't win, but because it never fights.

---

## HBeAg as the "anesthetic"

HBV also actively **feeds the immune system anesthetic**.

Chapter 2 mentioned that HBeAg isn't part of viral structure. It's actively secreted into the blood. One of its jobs is **inducing immune tolerance**.

- If the mother is HBeAg positive, HBeAg can cross the placenta into the fetus
- The fetal immune system, while developing, tags HBeAg as "one of us"
- Result: after birth, when HBV infection happens, the immune system doesn't recognize it

This is the **molecular-level reason** why mother-to-child vertical transmission leads to chronic infection.

---

## T cell exhaustion

Immune tolerance means "never launched." T cell exhaustion means "launched but worn out."

Normally, CD8+ T cells spot infected cells and jump on them. But in chronic HBV infection, T cells:

- Get constantly exposed to huge amounts of antigen
- Try to kill the virus over and over, failing each time
- Gradually **lose function**
- Start expressing a bunch of "brake molecules" on their surface: **PD-1, TIM-3, LAG-3**
- End up in a "half-dead" state — **exhaustion**

**The T cells aren't dead. They stepped on their own brakes.**

If those brakes can be released, T cells might get their killing power back.

🔑 That's why **PD-1 inhibitors** (the ones that made huge waves in cancer treatment) keep getting tested in HBV.

---

## Why do all those empty shells also help the virus

Chapter 2 mentioned that HBsAg empty shells in the blood outnumber complete virus particles by **10,000 to 1**.

These shells aren't infectious themselves, but they:

- **Burn through your antibodies** — antibodies neutralize empty shells, and real virus slips through
- **Keep numbing T cells** — antigen pressure is so high, T cells exhaust faster

**The virus doesn't fight hard. It uses "information overload" to wear the immune system out.**

That's why "lowering HBsAg" by itself has immunotherapy meaning — **take the antigen pressure off, and T cells get a chance to recover**.

The reason siRNA and ASO are exciting isn't just that they lower HBsAg numbers. It's that **after the drop, the immune system might get to "catch its breath."**

---

## Innate immunity gets suppressed too

Beyond T cells (adaptive immunity), HBV also plays tricks on **innate immunity**.

- After HBV infects a hepatocyte, it barely triggers any **interferon** response — it's like a "stealth plane"
- HBx protein can interfere with the host's viral sensing pathways
- NK cells (natural killer cells) also become functionally impaired in chronic infection

That's why interferon works in some patients — **it artificially supplies the signal the virus was secretly suppressing**.

---

## The four immune phases

Chronic HBV infection is clinically divided into four phases, reflecting the **battle state between virus and immunity**:

| Phase | Features | Immune state |
|------|------|---------|
| **Immune tolerance** | HBeAg+, high DNA, normal ALT | Pretending to sleep |
| **Immune clearance** | HBeAg+, high DNA, elevated ALT | Awake, fighting |
| **Low replication** | HBeAg-, low DNA, normal ALT | Done fighting, ceasefire |
| **Reactivation** | HBeAg-, DNA rebound | Virus mutated and escaped, war restarts |

One person can bounce between these phases throughout their life.

**The immune clearance phase is the most "dramatic" one** — ALT is high, liver damage is obvious, but it's also the phase with the best chance of spontaneous seroconversion. So doctors are very cautious about intervening here — **stepping in too early can actually suppress the immune response**.

---

## The immunology goal of a cure

If you translate "cure" into immunology language, it really means:

> **Get the immune system to recognize HBV as "the enemy" again, and clear infected cells.**

Three directions:

1. **Reduce antigen pressure** — siRNA / ASO to lower HBsAg
2. **Release the T cell brakes** — PD-1 inhibitors
3. **Bring in new soldiers** — therapeutic vaccines, teach the immune system to relearn

These three lines form the core of Part V on immunotherapy.

---

## 📍 Key Points

- The core of chronic HBV isn't a weak immune system. It's **immune tolerance + T cell exhaustion**
- Newborn infection makes the immune system mistake HBV for "one of us"
- HBeAg is the "anesthetic" that maintains tolerance
- Empty shell particles burn through antibodies and speed up T cell exhaustion
- Immunology goal of a cure: **take off antigen pressure, release the brakes, bring in soldiers**
