# Appendix E · Patient 100 Questions

> All the questions you want to ask but may feel awkward asking your doctor.

---

## Transmission and Daily Contact

**Q1. Can it spread through sharing a meal?**
A: **No.** With an intact oral mucosa, HBV virtually never spreads through shared meals. China's traditional "individual serving" habit is a good practice, but not mandatory.

**Q2. Can it spread through kissing?**
A: **Generally no.** HBV concentration in saliva is extremely low. But if both parties have oral wounds or ulcers, there is a theoretical tiny risk.

**Q3. Can it spread through hugging or shaking hands?**
A: **Absolutely not.**

**Q4. What about sharing toilets, towels, or utensils?**
A: **No.** HBV requires **blood-to-blood or body-fluid-to-blood** contact.

**Q5. What about sharing razors, nail clippers, or toothbrushes?**
A: **Risky.** These tools may carry blood residue. **Do not share them.**

**Q6. Can mosquito bites transmit it?**
A: **No.** Viruses in mosquito saliva are digested.

**Q7. What about blood donation?**
A: **HBsAg-positive people cannot donate blood.** People who have recovered (Anti-HBs positive, Anti-HBc negative) can in theory, but many blood banks still refuse them.

**Q8. What about cosmetic surgery, tattoos, or ear piercings?**
A: **Only go to reputable places with strict instrument sterilization.** You can also proactively inform staff about your HBV status so they can take precautions.

## Marriage, Reproduction, and Family

**Q9. Can I get married?**
A: **Yes.** Once your spouse completes the vaccination, they are protected.

**Q10. How do I protect my spouse?**
A: Have them get the HBV vaccine and check that Anti-HBs ≥ 10 mIU/mL, which confers protection.

**Q11. Will it spread when I give birth?**
A: **Mother-to-child transmission is the main route, but it can be effectively blocked.** Vaccinate the newborn within 12 hours of birth + HBIG (hepatitis B immunoglobulin), and the blocking rate is > 95%.

**Q12. What if the mother has a high viral load?**
A: Start **TDF** in late pregnancy (weeks 24-28) to further reduce transmission risk, with a blocking rate > 99%.

**Q13. Is breastfeeding safe?**
A: **Safe, provided the newborn has been properly vaccinated and given HBIG.** Major global guidelines recommend breastfeeding.

**Q14. C-section or vaginal delivery, which is better?**
A: The mode of delivery **does not affect** HBV mother-to-child transmission risk (when modern blocking is in place). Choose C-section or vaginal delivery based on other medical indications.

**Q15. Dad has hepatitis B, mom is healthy, can they have a child?**
A: **Yes.** Father-to-child transmission is not a major route. Just vaccinate the child on the standard schedule.

**Q16. Will it be inherited by my child?**
A: **HBV is not a genetic disease.** It is transmitted through blood/body fluids. Children are generally safe after vaccination.

**Q17. I'm pregnant. Can I continue taking TAF?**
A: TAF data are limited. It's recommended to **switch to TDF during pregnancy** (Pregnancy Category B, with extensive safety data).

**Q18. Will IVF (in vitro fertilization) transmit it?**
A: **Generally no.** A reputable reproductive medicine center will handle the viral aspects. Just inform your doctor of your status.

## Symptoms and Signs

**Q19. Will hepatitis B make me feel tired?**
A: Most chronic carriers **have no obvious symptoms.** Fatigue may occur with elevated ALT or cirrhosis.

**Q20. Are yellow eyes a sign that liver cancer is imminent?**
A: **Not necessarily.** Yellowing of the whites of the eyes (jaundice) indicates elevated bilirubin, which may reflect an acute drop in liver function and warrants immediate medical attention — but many other causes can also produce it (gallstones, medications, etc.).

**Q21. Is abdominal bloating a sign of cirrhosis?**
A: **Not necessarily.** But if it is accompanied by increasing abdominal girth and leg edema, evaluation for ascites is needed, and decompensated cirrhosis should be considered.

**Q22. Why have I been so tired recently?**
A: Fatigue in chronic disease can be psychological, metabolic, from anemia, thyroid issues, etc. **Do a comprehensive workup first; don't immediately attribute it to hepatitis B.**

**Q23. Is itchy skin related to hepatitis B?**
A: Severe liver disease (especially cholestasis) can cause itching. But mild itching is not necessarily hepatitis B.

## Medication-Related

**Q24. Is it serious if I miss a dose?**
A: **One missed dose is not a big deal.** Take it as soon as you remember. Repeated missed doses are the real problem.

**Q25. Will the medication cause hair loss?**
A: **Nucleoside drugs generally do not.** Interferon can.

**Q26. Will the medication affect sexual function?**
A: First-line nucleoside drugs basically don't. A minority of patients have psychosomatic reactions. **If it bothers you, tell your doctor.**

**Q27. Can I take Panadol for a cold?**
A: **Yes, but no more than 2 grams per day.** Stricter for those with severe liver disease.

**Q28. Can I take painkillers?**
A: Short-term acetaminophen (Tylenol/Panadol) is safe. **Avoid long-term high-dose NSAIDs (ibuprofen, etc.)**, especially for cirrhosis patients.

**Q29. What if I get COVID-19?**
A: **Treat it normally; hepatitis B is not a high-risk factor.** Continue your antiviral, and standard COVID drugs (Paxlovid, etc.) mostly do not conflict with TAF/TDF/ETV.

**Q30. Is the COVID vaccine safe?**
A: **Safe.** Chronic hepatitis B is not a contraindication for the COVID vaccine.

## Lifestyle

**Q31. Can I drink alcohol?**
A: The recommendation is **no alcohol at all**, especially if you already have fibrosis.

**Q32. What about coffee?**
A: **You can, and it may be beneficial.** 2-3 cups a day, no sugar.

**Q33. Can I eat spicy food?**
A: Yes. **There is no scientific evidence that "spicy food harms the liver."**

**Q34. Can I eat seafood?**
A: Yes. **Fresh** seafood is fine. Avoid raw seafood (especially for cirrhosis patients, be aware of Vibrio vulnificus).

**Q35. Can I work out or run?**
A: **Yes, encouraged.** Unless during an acute hepatitis flare.

**Q36. Can I dive?**
A: Yes, as long as you pass a routine physical.

**Q37. Can I travel abroad?**
A: **Yes.** Bring enough medication and get any vaccines recommended for your destination.

**Q38. What are the effects of staying up late?**
A: Long-term late nights worsen liver injury. Try to keep a regular schedule.

**Q39. Can I get a tattoo?**
A: Yes, but go to a reputable, properly sterilized place, and inform them of your hepatitis B status.

**Q40. Can I dye my hair?**
A: Yes. Hair dye has no direct relationship with hepatitis B.

## Prognosis and Risk

**Q41. How long can I live?**
A: **Chronic hepatitis B patients on standard therapy have a life expectancy close to the general population.** Untreated high-risk patients (high viral load, fibrosis) have a worse prognosis.

**Q42. Will I get cirrhosis?**
A: Untreated chronic hepatitis B progresses to cirrhosis in about 20-30% of patients within 20 years. **Standard treatment significantly lowers this.**

**Q43. Will I get liver cancer?**
A: The annual HCC incidence for chronic hepatitis B is about 0.5-1%, and 3-8% for cirrhosis. **Regular screening is key.**

**Q44. If there's liver cancer in my family, am I at higher risk?**
A: Yes. **A first-degree relative's history of liver cancer roughly doubles your HCC risk.** You need closer monitoring.

**Q45. My HBeAg has turned negative. Am I cured?**
A: It's a good thing, but **not a cure.** You may have entered the "low-replicative phase" or "HBeAg-negative chronic hepatitis." Continue monitoring.

**Q46. Is HBsAg seroconversion a cure?**
A: It is a **functional cure.** cccDNA may still be present, and liver cancer monitoring is still needed.

**Q47. My ALT has always been normal. Does that mean I'm fine?**
A: **Not necessarily.** In the immune-tolerant phase with normal ALT + high DNA, the liver may still be slowly damaged. A comprehensive evaluation is more reliable.

## Doctors and Visits

**Q48. What department should I see?**
A: **Hepatology** (if available), **gastroenterology**, or **infectious disease**. Most general hospitals have dedicated hepatitis B clinics.

**Q49. Do I need to be hospitalized?**
A: **Generally no.** Unless there is an acute hepatitis flare, decompensated liver function, HCC treatment, etc.

**Q50. How often should I follow up?**
A: For those on stable treatment, every 3-6 months. For cirrhosis, every 3-4 months. For the first 6 months of an off-treatment observation period, monthly.

**Q51. What if I move to a different city for care?**
A: **Bring your full medical records, lab reports, and prior imaging.** The new doctor may ask for a baseline re-check.

**Q52. What if I change doctors?**
A: Perfectly fine. Doctors and patients also need to be a "good match."

## Psychology and Society

**Q53. What if I'm very anxious?**
A: Allow yourself to be anxious, and at the same time find ways to cope. **Counseling, family support, and community exchange all help.**

**Q54. Should I tell my family?**
A: **Yes, tell your spouse, parents, and adult children.** Help them get vaccinated and understand the basics.

**Q55. Should I tell my friends?**
A: Entirely up to you. **Hepatitis B is not a disease to feel ashamed of.**

**Q56. Will I face discrimination when looking for a job?**
A: **Chinese law explicitly prohibits employment discrimination based on hepatitis B** (the Employment Promotion Law). **Except for a very few professions (direct food contact, some military/police roles), physical exams cannot block hiring due to hepatitis B.**

**Q57. What about insurance?**
A: China: Health insurance may charge a premium or exclude coverage for hepatitis B. **Buying insurance before diagnosis is wise.**
US and Canada: Follow local regulations; under the ACA, chronic conditions cannot be denied coverage.

## History of Infection and Screening

**Q58. When was I infected?**
A: Most chronic infections in China occur during the **perinatal period or early childhood.** For adult infections, the chronicity rate is < 5%.

**Q59. Should the rest of my family be tested?**
A: **We strongly recommend family screening for the two-and-a-half panel.** Especially parents (a potential source of infection) and siblings.

**Q60. My child has been vaccinated. Does he still need testing?**
A: If newborn vaccination + mother-to-child blocking were done properly, generally no. **If you're worried, a single Anti-HBs test to confirm the presence of protective antibodies is enough.**

**Q61. I was vaccinated. Why did I still get infected?**
A: Possible reasons: (1) already infected at the time of vaccination (vaccine ineffective); (2) failed to develop antibodies after vaccination; (3) antibodies faded and were followed by a high-risk exposure.

**Q62. My Anti-HBs is positive. Do I still need a booster?**
A: **Generally no.** As long as protective antibodies are ≥ 10 mIU/mL, that's enough.

**Q63. My Anti-HBs used to be positive but is now negative. What should I do?**
A: Antibodies may fade, but **immune memory is usually still there** — you can still rapidly produce antibodies upon exposure. You can get one booster shot.

## Specific Medications

**Q64. Which is better, TAF or TDF?**
A: **TAF has fewer kidney/bone side effects but is more expensive; TDF is cheaper, but monitor kidney and bone with long-term use.** It depends on your age, comorbidities, and finances.

**Q65. Should entecavir be taken on an empty stomach?**
A: Yes, **no food for 2 hours before and after.** Or take it at bedtime.

**Q66. Can I buy hepatitis B drugs online myself?**
A: **Not recommended.** Always go through a reputable hospital or pharmacy, with proper prescription management.

**Q67. Are generic drugs and originator drugs equally effective?**
A: **Mainstream generics are essentially just as effective, and much cheaper.** Domestic Chinese TDF and ETV generics are already widely used.

**Q68. Can traditional Chinese medicine cure hepatitis B?**
A: **It cannot replace standard antiviral treatment.** A few have adjunctive anti-inflammatory effects, but **cannot clear HBsAg.**

## Surgery and Other Medical Care

**Q69. Do I need to tell my doctor before surgery?**
A: **You must.** The doctor will take precautions and assess whether your liver function can tolerate surgery.

**Q70. Do I need to stop my medication for a tooth extraction?**
A: **No need to stop hepatitis B medication.** Just inform the dentist of your status.

**Q71. Are gastroscopy and colonoscopy risky?**
A: **Generally safe.** Endoscopy equipment is strictly sterilized.

**Q72. Does general anesthesia affect hepatitis B?**
A: **Generally no.** Liver function is assessed before surgery; caution is needed for those with severe liver damage.

**Q73. What about chemotherapy?**
A: **HBV screening must be done before chemotherapy!** HBsAg-positive + chemo = major risk of viral reactivation, which can cause fulminant hepatitis. **Prophylactic antiviral therapy (TAF/TDF/ETV) is usually needed.**

**Q74. What about biologics like rituximab or TNF inhibitors?**
A: Similar to chemotherapy — **antivirals must be used before, during, and after treatment.**

## Special Situations

**Q75. What if I also have fatty liver?**
A: **You must treat both conditions together.** Control weight, diet, and exercise. Fatty liver accelerates fibrosis and HCC.

**Q76. What if I also have diabetes?**
A: Glucose control = liver protection. **HBV + diabetes = 3-4x HCC risk.**

**Q77. What if I have HCV/HDV co-infection?**
A: You need **specialist evaluation.** Treatment plans are completely different. Co-infection carries higher risk.

**Q78. What if I have HIV co-infection?**
A: TAF/TDF cover both HBV and HIV. **See an infectious disease specialist to build an integrated regimen.**

**Q79. What about hepatitis A on top?**
A: Superimposed hepatitis A on top of hepatitis B can cause severe disease. **Get the hepatitis A vaccine promptly.**

## Future and New Drugs

**Q80. Should I wait for new drugs or start treatment now?**
A: **Not mutually exclusive — start treatment now, and keep an eye on new drugs.** Not taking medication while waiting for new drugs is the worst choice.

**Q81. When will new drugs be available?**
A: The first may be Bepirovirsen (2027-2028). Combination regimens will be approved over the next 5-10 years.

**Q82. Can I join a clinical trial?**
A: Depends on the criteria. Check ClinicalTrials.gov, or ask your doctor.

**Q83. Are trials risky?**
A: Yes — investigational drugs may have unknown side effects. **Read the informed consent form carefully.**

**Q84. Do trials cost money?**
A: Legitimate trials **do not charge fees.** Any that pay you are the exception.

**Q85. I can't stop my medication. Will new drugs let me stop?**
A: **Possibly.** One goal of new drugs is "finite-duration therapy with durable off-treatment control."

## Mindset and Philosophy

**Q86. Am I an "unclean person"?**
A: **Absolutely not.** You are a person coexisting with a virus.

**Q87. Am I a burden on my family?**
A: **Absolutely not.** Your family's support is part of your recovery journey, not a burden.

**Q88. What if I'm worried about suddenly getting liver cancer?**
A: **Regular monitoring is the best insurance.** Early-stage HCC survival rate is > 70%.

**Q89. Should I tell my girlfriend/boyfriend?**
A: **Once the relationship gets serious, tell them.** Hiding it is not a long-term solution.

**Q90. Will I be abandoned?**
A: Some people will accept it; some will not. That's not your fault — it's **a matter of compatibility in the relationship.**

**Q91. I'm afraid to have children. What should I do?**
A: **Mother-to-child blocking is very mature.** Modern medicine can keep your child nearly 100% healthy.

## Common Misconceptions

**Q92. "Little three positives are easier to treat than big three positives"?**
A: **Not necessarily.** Both must be judged comprehensively based on DNA, ALT, and fibrosis.

**Q93. "Normal liver function means nothing to worry about"?**
A: **Wrong.** In the immune-tolerant phase, ALT is normal but the virus is actively replicating, and progression can be silent.

**Q94. "Once vaccinated, I'm fine"?**
A: The vaccine prevents **future** infection. Those already infected need antiviral treatment.

**Q95. "Turning into little three positives means I'm cured"?**
A: **No.** Little three positives is just a change in staging, not a cure.

**Q96. "As long as DNA is negative, I'm fine"?**
A: **While HBsAg is still positive, it doesn't count as a cure.** Stopping treatment may cause a rebound, and liver cancer risk remains.

## Overall

**Q97. What is the single most important thing when you have this disease?**
A: **Standard treatment + regular monitoring + staying updated on progress.** Do these three well, and you're already ahead of most patients.

**Q98. Summarize this book in one sentence?**
A: **HBV is hard to cure but is being conquered. You live in the most hopeful era — standard treatment + keep hope + keep learning.**

**Q99. If I do only one thing, what should it be?**
A: **Don't stop your medication.**

**Q100. Will there really be a cure in the future?**
A: **Personally, I believe yes.** But even if a cure isn't found, you can still live healthy, dignified, and with self-respect — something patients decades ago could not have imagined.
