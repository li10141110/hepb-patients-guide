# Ch 14 · Who Needs Treatment: Comparing Three Major Guidelines

> The same lab report can get different answers in different countries.

---

## Why this chapter matters

If you're a Chinese person living abroad, or you've seen doctors in different cities, you've probably run into this —

**"The Shanghai doctor said treat. The Canadian doctor said watch and wait."**

It's not about right or wrong. It's that **the guidelines themselves are different**.

The four major guidelines:

- **AASLD** (American Association for the Study of Liver Diseases)
- **EASL** (European Association for the Study of the Liver)
- **APASL** (Asia-Pacific Association for the Study of the Liver)
- **China 2022 Chronic Hepatitis B Guidelines**

The core disagreement: **which patients should be treated?**

---

## Big-picture consensus (all guidelines agree)

Start with what they all agree on:

**All guidelines agree → treat immediately:**

- **Cirrhosis** (compensated or decompensated)
- **Acute liver failure**
- **Personal or family history of HCC** (with HBV positive)
- **About to receive immunosuppression** (chemo, transplant, biologics)

**These aren't controversial. The controversy is with patients in the "middle zone."**

---

## The main debate: when to treat

The debate centers on two thresholds:

1. **HBV DNA threshold** (how high before treating?)
2. **ALT threshold** (how high before treating?)

**AASLD 2018**

- HBeAg positive: **DNA > 2 × 10⁴ + ALT > 2 × ULN**
- HBeAg negative: **DNA > 2 × 10³ + ALT > 2 × ULN**
- Or ≥ F2 fibrosis

**EASL 2017**

- **DNA > 2000 + high ALT + at least moderate fibrosis (or inflammation)**
- More conservative, insists on "all three together"

**APASL 2015/2022**

- Lower thresholds, leans toward earlier treatment
- DNA > 2000 + high ALT is enough to start

**China 2022**

- The most recent revision **broadly expanded who gets treated**
- If **HBV DNA is positive + ALT is persistently abnormal**, treatment is recommended
- In some situations, **ALT normal but age > 30 + DNA positive + family history of cirrhosis/HCC**, treatment is also recommended
- **Intervening in the immune tolerance phase is being discussed**

---

## Why is the Chinese guideline the most aggressive?

Three reasons:

**① China has a huge HBV population** — 70 million cases in the pool. Passive observation would translate into a massive absolute number of liver cancer cases.

**② Chinese patients are mostly genotype B/C** — especially C, which progresses fast and carries higher HCC risk.

**③ Antivirals are already very safe** — long-term TAF has minor side effects, so the treatment bar can be lower.

**The Chinese guideline's philosophy: "If you can treat, treat. Treating won't hurt."**

---

## A concrete example

Suppose: 32-year-old woman, HBeAg negative, HBV DNA 3000 IU/mL, ALT 45 U/L, FibroScan 6.2 kPa, no family history of HCC.

- **AASLD**: no treatment (ALT is not > 2×ULN)
- **EASL**: watch, no immediate treatment
- **APASL**: can start treatment
- **China 2022**: recommends treatment

**Same person, four guidelines, four answers.**

**What do you do?**

- If you're in the US, your doctor will most likely say "watch"
- If you're in China, they'll most likely tell you to start meds

**At that point, your decision isn't based on "which doctor is right." It's based on — what long-term risk and drug burden you personally want to accept.**

---

## My personal view

This is my **personal view**. Not guideline.

For **young patients**:

- Antivirals are already very safe
- Early treatment significantly cuts long-term HCC risk
- The psychological burden of a lifetime of monitoring > the burden of long-term meds
- **I lean toward the Chinese guideline's stance — treat early**

For **older patients**:

- Key windows have already been missed
- Drug clearance burden is higher
- Assessment should be more conservative

**But everyone is different. This decision has to be made together with your doctor.**

---

## Pre-treatment checklist

If you decide to start treatment, confirm these first:

- [ ] HBV DNA + HBsAg quantitation + HBeAg / Anti-HBe (baseline)
- [ ] ALT + AST + albumin + bilirubin + PT
- [ ] FibroScan or FIB-4
- [ ] Abdominal ultrasound + AFP
- [ ] Creatinine, kidney function (TDF vs TAF choice)
- [ ] Bone density baseline (if older patient)
- [ ] Rule out coexisting HCV, HDV, HIV (especially newly diagnosed)
- [ ] Pregnancy status (women)
- [ ] Family HCC history
- [ ] Prior drug resistance history

---

## Follow-up rhythm after starting treatment

Densest in the first year, thinning out later:

- **Month 1** — ALT, creatinine (early safety)
- **Month 3** — DNA + HBsAg quantitation + ALT
- **Month 6** — full liver panel + DNA + HBsAg + AFP + ultrasound
- **Every 6 months after** — routine follow-up
- **Every 12 months** — FibroScan
- **Cirrhotic patients** — tighter interval, every 3-4 months

---

## Who shouldn't be on antivirals?

A few less common situations:

- **Young patients in the immune tolerance phase (< 30)** — controversial; some guidelines still favor watching
- **Transient ALT spike but low DNA** — repeat and rule out other causes first
- **Pregnant women** — unless high viral load or transmission prevention is needed, usually start TDF in mid-to-late pregnancy
- **Breastfeeding** — TDF is relatively safe; less data on TAF

---

## The one-line principle

> **Whether to treat isn't decided by any single number. Look at the combination — virus + liver damage + fibrosis + family history + age — plus what you personally are willing to take on.**

---

## 📍 Key Points

- All guidelines agree: cirrhosis, HCC history, before immunosuppression → **must treat**
- The debate is in the middle zone, especially borderline ALT + moderate DNA
- **China 2022 is the most aggressive**, expanding treatment eligibility
- Same patient, different guideline, potentially different advice
- **The final decision is between you and your doctor**
- A thorough baseline evaluation before starting treatment matters
