# Ch 11 · What HBV DNA and HBsAg Quantitation Mean

> What's behind the number is what tells you whether to worry.

---

## Two numbers, two different things

New patients almost always ask about two numbers on their report:

- **HBV DNA: how much?** (viral load)
- **HBsAg: how much?** (surface antigen quantitation)

They mean **different things**.

- **HBV DNA** = how much virus is being produced right now
- **HBsAg** = how much surface protein is being produced right now (cccDNA + integrated)

One is activity. The other is "inventory depth."

---

## HBV DNA: how active the virus is

Units: IU/mL (International Units per milliliter).

Rough scale:

| Range | State |
|---------|---------|
| < 20 (undetectable) | Treatment has suppressed it |
| 20-2000 | Low activity |
| 2000-10⁵ | Medium |
| 10⁵-10⁸ | High activity |
| > 10⁸ | Very high (common in immune tolerance phase or acute infection) |

**A few concrete points:**

- In untreated patients, DNA levels don't map neatly onto liver damage — during **immune tolerance**, DNA is often > 10⁷ but ALT is normal
- Treatment goal: push it to **undetectable**
- During treatment, DNA sometimes "blips" (small swings within 1-2 log): **some researchers call this a blip, and it usually doesn't mean switching drugs**
- If it keeps climbing: think about **resistance** or **adherence issues**

---

## HBsAg quantitation: the core number on the road to cure

The meaning of HBsAg quantitation has been completely reshaped in the last 5 years.

Before — **"HBsAg positive is positive, the number doesn't matter much."**
Now — **"How fast HBsAg quantitation drops is the primary endpoint for evaluating new drugs."**

Why?

Because functional cure (HBsAg turning negative) has to go through a drop in quantitation first. And how fast it drops reflects:

- How much cccDNA transcription is suppressed
- How much integrated DNA is expressing
- How deep the drug's effect goes

**HBsAg quantitation scale:**

| Range (IU/mL) | Meaning |
|-------------|------|
| > 10⁴ | High, common starting point for new drug trials |
| 10³ - 10⁴ | Medium |
| 100 - 10³ | Low |
| < 100 | **Threshold zone for functional cure** |
| < 10 | Very low, chance of spontaneous or drug-induced clearance rises significantly |
| < 0.05 | Undetectable, meets functional cure criteria |

**Pay special attention to the 100 IU/mL threshold.**

Lots of data show that once HBsAg **drops below 100 IU/mL**, spontaneous or interferon-induced clearance rates go up a lot. This is called the **"HBsAg cliff"** — **once you're past it, the chance of cure jumps sharply**.

**One key endpoint for new drug trials is: "HBsAg drops below 100 and stays there for 24 weeks."**

---

## The rate of decline matters too

A single number tells you only so much. The **trend** matters more.

Three patterns:

**A. Plateau** — bounces around 1000-3000 for years. Most common in patients on nucleoside drugs. Reflects integrated DNA dominance.

**B. Slow decline** — drops 0.1-0.3 log per year. Typical natural progression.

**C. Rapid decline** — more than 1 log drop in half a year. Seen in:
- Responders to interferon
- Patients on siRNA / ASO therapy
- Immune flare (with rising ALT)

**In new drug trials, the fast decliners are the ones with the best shot at functional cure.**

---

## A counterintuitive picture: low DNA, high HBsAg

Many long-term-treated patients look like this:

- DNA is undetectable
- HBsAg is still 1000+

This is a **virological success, immunological failure** — DNA is under control, but antigens keep pumping out, and the immune system stays "information-overloaded."

This is exactly the clinical face of the shift described in Ch 8 — "HBsAg source migrating to integrated DNA."

**New drugs are aimed at this step.**

---

## Things that can distort HBsAg quantitation

A few situations can make HBsAg quantitation "unreal":

- **Different kits give different results** — recheck at the same lab
- **Genotype effect** — B/C genotypes express HBsAg differently
- **Acute infection window** — can spike briefly in the early phase
- **HBsAg mutants** — some escape mutations may be **missed** by standard kits
- **Above upper detection limit** — need dilution and rerun

When tracking a trend, stick to the **same lab and same kit**.

---

## What HBeAg quantitation adds

Clinically, most reports just say "positive/negative," but **HBeAg quantitation** is being used in more places.

- Falling HBeAg quantitation → may be approaching seroconversion
- On interferon, HBeAg quantitation < 10 suggests a good response

Not every hospital runs quantitation. If you're in a new drug trial, they may ask for it.

---

## How viral quantitation drives decisions

**Whether to start treatment** — mainly DNA + ALT + fibrosis + family history. HBsAg quantitation is a reference.

**Which regimen to pick** — high DNA calls for TAF/TDF/ETV; low DNA may allow observation.

**Evaluating treatment response** — is DNA suppressed, is HBsAg dropping.

**Deciding when to stop** — DNA undetectable is only necessary; **HBsAg quantitation < 100 or negative** is a stronger indicator.

**Estimating HCC risk** — high HBsAg quantitation (> 10⁴) is linked to higher HCC risk.

---

## A quick word on mindset

Numbers fluctuate. Don't panic over one bad result.

- HBV DNA can swing 1-2 log naturally
- HBsAg quantitation can differ across labs
- A single ALT spike isn't necessarily bad (could be the immune system fighting)

**Watch trends, not single points.**

---

## 📍 Key Points

- **HBV DNA** = current viral activity; **HBsAg quantitation** = total antigen inventory
- HBV DNA goal: **push it to undetectable**
- HBsAg quantitation **< 100 IU/mL** is the "cure cliff" — cross it and clearance rates jump
- Core endpoint in new drug trials is often "HBsAg dropped below 100 and sustained"
- After long-term treatment, **DNA negative but HBsAg high** is typical — reflects integrated DNA dominance
- HBV RNA / HBcrAg are supplementary new markers
- **Trends over single points**
